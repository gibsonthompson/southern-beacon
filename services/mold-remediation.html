Remediation Means Removal, Not Treatment

The standard is unambiguous on this point: physical removal of contamination is the primary means of remediation. Spraying an antimicrobial over mold growth and leaving it in place does not remediate anything. Dead spores remain allergenic, and the material that fed the colony is still in your wall. Encapsulants and biocides have narrow, legitimate uses after removal is complete. They are not a substitute for it.

We work to ANSI/IICRC S520, the American National Standard for Professional Mold Remediation. It defines how contamination is classified, how the work area is isolated, how materials are removed, and how completion is confirmed. Following it is what separates remediation from cleaning.

How the Standard Classifies Your Situation

S520 describes three conditions. Condition 1 is normal fungal ecology, a space whose spore profile matches the outdoors. Condition 2 is settled spores and dust from a nearby source without growth of its own. Condition 3 is actual mold growth. The goal of every project is to return the affected area to Condition 1, and the classification drives how aggressive the containment and cleaning need to be.

Project scale matters as much as condition. The EPA treats roughly ten square feet as the line between a homeowner-manageable cleanup and professional work. Areas beyond one hundred square feet call for full containment, engineering controls, and formal verification.

What a Project Includes

  • Containment sized to the contamination, sealing the work area from the rest of the building
  • Negative air pressure with HEPA-filtered air scrubbers so contaminated air cannot migrate into clean space
  • Sealing or deactivating HVAC returns and supplies serving the work area
  • Physical removal of porous materials that cannot be cleaned, cut back to sound material and bagged inside containment
  • HEPA vacuuming and detail cleaning of framing, semi-porous, and non-porous surfaces
  • Correction of the moisture source, because remediation without it is temporary by definition
  • Structural drying with moisture readings logged until materials reach dry standard
  • Post-remediation verification before containment comes down

Drying to a Standard, Not a Feeling

Wood that reads above roughly sixteen percent moisture content will support fungal growth again. Surfaces can feel dry to the hand while the framing behind them holds enough water to restart the problem, which is why we monitor with meters and log readings daily rather than judging by appearance.

The Part Most Companies Skip

A remediation invoice proves work was performed. It does not prove the work succeeded. Verification, covered in detail on our clearance testing page, is the difference between a clean-looking room and a documented return to Condition 1, and it is the only record that holds up with an insurer or a future buyer.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to move out during remediation?+
Usually not. Proper containment and negative air isolate the work area from the rest of the home. For extensive contamination, work involving the HVAC system, or households with significant respiratory sensitivities, we will discuss whether relocating for part of the project makes sense.
How long does remediation take?+
A contained single-room project is often one to three days including drying. Larger projects with extensive demolition and structural drying run a week or more. Your written scope includes the expected timeline.
Can you just spray something to kill it?+
No, and any company offering that is selling you a cosmetic result. Killing mold does not remove the allergenic material or the substrate it grew on. Physical removal is the standard of care.
