What Is Mold, Really?

No fear tactics. Here is what mold is, what it needs to grow, and how to tell an actual problem from a stain.

Call (470) 760-5249 Book a Free Walk-Through

A Fungus Doing Its Job in the Wrong Place

Mold is a fungus. In nature it is the reason a fallen tree eventually disappears into the forest floor, breaking down dead organic material so the nutrients return to the soil. There are over one hundred thousand known species, and only a few dozen commonly show up indoors. Spores are in the air you are breathing right now, indoors and out, and there is no practical way to make a building spore-free. That is not the goal and any company promising it is misleading you.

Mold becomes a problem when spores land on a material they can eat, in conditions wet enough to let them germinate, and nobody interrupts it. Then a few spores become a colony, the colony releases far more spores than the background level, and the indoor environment stops resembling the outdoor one. That imbalance, not the mere presence of spores, is what remediation corrects.

Four Things It Needs

  • Moisture. The only one you can realistically control, which is why every legitimate remediation ends with correcting a water source.
  • Food. Anything with cellulose: drywall paper, wood framing, cardboard, carpet backing, insulation facing, even household dust on a hard surface.
  • Temperature. Most indoor species are comfortable in the same range you are, roughly 60 to 80 degrees, so a Georgia house is hospitable year round.
  • Time. Growth can begin within 24 to 48 hours of materials getting wet, which is why response speed after a leak matters so much.

Why Georgia Is Different

Our climate does two things that work against you. Summer humidity routinely sits at levels where materials pick up enough moisture from the air alone to support the least demanding species, no leak required. And our red clay holds stormwater against foundations rather than draining it, so basements and crawlspaces stay damp long after the weather clears.

That combination is why so much metro Atlanta growth shows up in basements, crawlspaces, and closets on exterior walls rather than the dramatic burst-pipe scenarios people picture.

The Health Question, Answered Honestly

Mold exposure is associated with allergic reactions, asthma attacks, and other respiratory complaints. Children, older adults, people with existing respiratory conditions, and anyone immunocompromised generally react sooner and more severely. Those associations are well established and worth taking seriously.

What we will not tell you is that a particular species in your wall caused a particular symptom you are experiencing. No air sample can establish that, and a remediation company claiming to diagnose you has left its lane. Our job is to document what is in the building, remove it correctly, and eliminate the water. What that exposure means for your health is a conversation for your physician, and we are glad to hand over the lab report for them to review.

When It Is Actually a Problem

Not every dark spot needs a contractor. Discoloration is frequently mineral staining, soot from a candle or fireplace, old water marks with no active growth, or a thin surface mildew on bathroom grout that a homeowner can clean. The EPA treats roughly ten square feet as the line where professional help makes sense.

It is worth a professional look when the area is larger than that, when it keeps returning after cleaning, when there is a musty odor with no visible source, when growth follows a known leak or flood, when it is inside an HVAC system, or when someone in the house has symptoms that improve away from home.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I ever get rid of all the mold in my house?+
No, and that is not the objective. Spores exist everywhere. The goal is returning your indoor environment to normal fungal ecology, meaning the spore types and concentrations indoors resemble the outdoor air rather than showing amplification from an indoor source.
Does mold always smell?+
Not always. The musty odor comes from compounds released during active growth, so a dormant or dried colony can be nearly odorless while still being present and still releasing allergenic material when disturbed.
Is a little mold in the shower a problem?+
Usually not. Surface growth on grout and caulk in a wet room is common and generally manageable with routine cleaning and better ventilation. It becomes our business when it returns immediately, spreads beyond the wet zone, or appears on the wall outside the shower, which suggests moisture inside the assembly.
